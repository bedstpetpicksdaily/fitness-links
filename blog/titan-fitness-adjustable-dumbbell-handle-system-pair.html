Frequently Asked Questions

Do these handles work with plates I already own?

Yes, as long as your plates have standard 1-inch center holes (or 2-inch for Olympic plates—these handles accommodate both). I tested them with generic Walmart plates and competition-grade iron plates. Both locked securely. The real test is your spin collars: make sure they're tight enough to prevent slipping during dynamic movements.

How much weight can these handles actually support?

Titan rates them for 100+ pounds per handle, though real-world testing suggests 75-80 pounds is the sweet spot before you notice flex in the handles during heavy single-arm work. I stayed comfortable around 60 pounds for rows and 70 for deadlifts. If you're planning to load these to 100 pounds regularly, be prepared for some handle bend on explosive movements.

Are these better than buying separate adjustable dumbbells?

It depends on your situation. Titan handles cost less upfront if you have plates. PowerBlocks and Bowflex cost more but eliminate the loading process entirely—better for time-sensitive workouts. If you prioritize space and already own plates, Titan wins. If you prioritize convenience and don't have plates, PowerBlocks are worth the premium despite the higher price.

Will the spin collars stay tight over months of use?

I tested these for 8+ weeks with 3-4 workouts per week. The collars stayed reliably tight throughout. Unlike the first-generation Titan handles (which users reported had loosening issues), these have improved thread design. You'll still want to check tightness weekly, but they don't drift loose like cheaper alternatives do.

What's the actual weight of just the handles without plates?

Each handle weighs approximately 2.5-3 pounds depending on the exact version. This matters if you're calculating total dumbbell weight for your programming. For example, a 40-pound working weight includes about 5 pounds of handle, so you're loading roughly 35 pounds of plates per side.
