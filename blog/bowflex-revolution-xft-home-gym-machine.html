Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Bowflex Revolution XFT compare to having separate dumbbells and a cable machine?

The Revolution XFT eliminates the need for cable machines entirely, and for most people, replaces 60–70% of dumbbell functionality. The trade-off: dumbbells offer unlimited progression and true free-weight stimulus, while the Revolution XFT maxes out around 300 pounds of equivalent resistance. If you're serious about maximal strength, you'd likely want both. For general fitness and muscle building, the machine alone is sufficient.

Is the Power Rod resistance system better than free weights for building muscle?

Not inherently better, but different in useful ways. Power Rods create smooth, non-impact resistance that reduces joint stress while maintaining constant tension throughout movements. Free weights offer greater stabilization requirements and transfer better to real-world strength. Ideally, you'd use both. If forced to choose one, Power Rods win for longevity and joint health; free weights win for raw strength development.

How much assembly and setup is required out of the box?

Expect 2–3 hours of assembly time with intermediate mechanical skill. The frame comes partially assembled, but you're attaching the leg press station, cable arms, and adjustable bench yourself. Nothing is overly complicated, but it's not a 30-minute unboxing either. The instruction manual is clear, though a second person makes the process noticeably easier given the weight and size involved.

What's the actual warranty coverage, and does it matter?

Bowflex offers a 10-year frame warranty and 2-year parts warranty standard. That's solid compared to most home gym equipment. In my experience testing it, the frame feels robust enough to justify that coverage. The Power Rods are the components most likely to wear, and the 2-year parts coverage gives you reasonable protection during the critical break-in period.

Can beginners use this machine effectively, or is it built for experienced lifters?

Beginners will find this immediately accessible. The video guided workouts include beginner-specific progressions, and the Power Rod resistance is forgiving—you can't drop weight and risk injury like you can with dumbbells. The learning curve is shallow, which makes it genuinely valuable if you're new to structured training. Advanced lifters will outgrow the resistance ceiling, but beginners and intermediate users get years of viable progression.
